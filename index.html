Abstract

What emerges or is withheld in the demolition of a building in the city centre at the end of November 2019? To this day, the cause of a fire at the Bank of Lisbon in September 2018, which lasted for 3 days and lead to its implosion, has not been disclosed publicly despite a series of legal investigations implicating provincial government departments, city offices and the Johannesburg Emergency Services. The Bank of Lisbon housed various departments, including the Department of Health, when it started to burn. On the first day of the fire, three firemen lost their lives as they got trapped on the 23rd floor. It emerged that fire hydrants in the building were dry. Grievous issues of occupational health and safety were ignored for years, despite repeated complaints by office workers and labour unions. At the time of the fire, the building should not have been occupied as it did not meet basic compliance standards.

This contribution consists of three parts that evolve around the unfolding narrative: It begins with a video recording of the implosion, filmed from the evacuated student residency directly opposite the Bank of Lisbon. 4 continuous minutes have been edited from a static 2 hour take, which register the site, texture and sound of material dislocation, in which daylight is absorbed and only gradually releases a view. The video, a collaboration with Simon Gush and Andrei van Wyk, developed alongside a text that collates online information around the fire. The essay acts as a re-mediated, selective account of events, trauma and political forces at play, sourced mainly from available news reports and interviews. It circles around allegations of negligence, arson and the deferral of accountability by governing bodies. The attempt is to bring into relation data, actors, quantifications and measures, which do not conclude but approximate the preventable loss of 3 lives. The third part constitutes a sound piece by Andrei van Wyk, a re-composition of the cameras’ audio recordings that proposes a sonic meditation on the collapse of architecture, a search for a vertical movement through a now imagined space. Between these instances, which stand as independent moments and distinct collaborations in this research, is the attempt to think through Locard’s exchange principle, that ‘every contact leaves a trace’ ; to acknowledge the presence of each trace in the city’s nervous system, echoing towards an alternate, socially legitimate reality.
